1. Engine

High-performance engines ranging from 300cc to over 1000cc, providing exceptional power and speed.


1. Engine Configuration

  • Inline-Four (I4): Known for balance and smoothness (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Kawasaki Ninja ZX-10R).
  • V-Twin (V2): Strong mid-range torque (e.g., Ducati Panigale V2).
  • V4 Engines: Compact and powerful (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4, Honda RC213V-S).
  • Parallel Twin: Lightweight and compact, common in smaller superbikes.
  • Supercharged Engines: Extremely powerful (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja H2 series).

2. Displacement

  • Range: Typically 600cc to 1300cc.
  • Entry-level superbikes: 600-800cc.
  • Flagship superbikes: 1000cc+.
  • Hyperbikes: 1200cc-1400cc (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-14R).

3. Power Output

  • Horsepower (HP): 100 HP for entry-level, up to 220 HP for 1000cc superbikes, and over 300 HP for high-performance models.
  • Torque: Varies by engine configuration, with V-Twins offering higher torque at lower RPMs.

4. Cooling System

  • Liquid Cooling: Used for consistent performance and heat management during high-speed runs.

5. Valvetrain

  • DOHC: Dual Overhead Camshaft for precise valve timing.
  • Valve Count: 4 valves per cylinder for improved combustion efficiency.

6. Fuel System

  • Fuel Injection: Ensures precise fuel delivery for better performance.
  • Throttle-by-Wire: Electronic throttle control for smoother power delivery.

7. Performance Enhancements

  • Quickshifters: Clutchless gear shifts for faster acceleration.
  • Ride-by-Wire: Electronic throttle for precise engine response.
  • Variable Valve Timing (VVT): Optimizes performance across the RPM range.

8. Compression Ratio

  • High Compression Ratios: Commonly 13:1 or more for enhanced combustion and power output.

9. Exhaust System

  • Design: Lightweight and aerodynamic for optimal performance.
  • Materials: Titanium or carbon fiber for reduced weight.
  • Sound: Aggressive tone, compliant with noise regulations.

10. Superbike Engine Innovations

  • Superchargers/Turbochargers: For extreme power boosts (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja H2).
  • Hybrid Technology: Combining electric motors with combustion engines.
  • MotoGP-Inspired Tech: Features like seamless gearboxes and pneumatic valves.

2. Transmission

6-speed manual gearbox with wet multi-plate and slipper clutch for smooth and responsive gear changes.


1. Transmission Type

  • Manual Transmission: Most superbikes use a 6-speed manual gearbox for precise control over power delivery.
  • Clutchless Transmission: Race-inspired models feature quick-shift systems for clutchless gear changes.

2. Clutch System

  • Wet Multi-Plate Clutch: Provides durability and smooth operation under heavy loads.
  • Slipper Clutch: Prevents wheel lockup during aggressive downshifting, enhancing safety.
  • Hydraulic Clutch: Found in premium models, offering smoother lever feel compared to cable-operated systems.

3. Gear Ratios

  • Close-Ratio Gearing: Optimized for acceleration and high-speed performance.
  • Customizable Ratios: Adjustable sprockets and chains for tailored performance.

4. Quickshifters

  • Up Quickshifter: Allows clutchless upshifts by momentarily cutting engine power.
  • Down Quickshifter: Enables clutchless downshifts with electronic throttle blipping.

5. Gear Indicator

Most modern superbikes include a gear indicator on the digital display for easy reference during riding.


6. Transmission Cooling

Efficient lubrication systems ensure heat dissipation and consistent performance during aggressive riding.


7. Final Drive System

  • Chain Drive: Common in superbikes for its lightweight nature and ease of maintenance.
  • Belt Drive: Rare in superbikes but offers quieter operation.
  • Shaft Drive: Found in sport-touring bikes, prioritizing reliability and low maintenance.

8. Racing Features

  • Seamless Gearbox: Eliminates interruptions during gear shifts for racing efficiency.
  • Launch Control: Optimizes power delivery for quick starts.
  • Auto-Blip Systems: Automatically rev-matches during downshifts for smooth transitions.

9. Reverse Gear

Rare in superbikes, reverse gears are more common in heavyweight touring motorcycles.


10. Maintenance Aspects

  • Regular chain tension checks are necessary for optimal performance.
  • Specialized gearbox oil is used to reduce friction and improve cooling.

3. Fuel and Efficiency

Fuel-efficient design with a tank capacity of 12-17 liters, offering 15-25 km/l based on the riding style.


1. Fuel Tank Capacity

  • Range: Typically ranges from 12 to 17 liters, depending on the model.
  • Superbikes: Higher-end models often have smaller tank capacities for reduced weight, while still offering adequate range for road and track use.
  • Hyperbikes: Some high-performance bikes (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja H2) may have slightly larger tanks for more range.

2. Fuel Efficiency

  • Entry-Level Superbike: Fuel consumption can range from 15-25 km/l, depending on riding style and model.
  • Mid-Range Superbike: Typically achieves around 10-18 km/l under mixed riding conditions.
  • High-Performance Superbikes: May offer around 8-14 km/l, as their focus is more on power than efficiency.

3. Range per Full Tank

  • Standard Bikes: With a full tank of fuel, most bikes can cover between 180-300 km, depending on fuel tank size and efficiency.
  • High-Performance Models: These bikes generally have a shorter range, often between 150-200 km due to their smaller tanks and higher fuel consumption rates.

4. Fuel System

  • Fuel Injection: Modern superbikes are equipped with electronic fuel injection (EFI) systems for better fuel efficiency and smoother power delivery.
  • Throttle-by-Wire: This system allows for more precise fuel delivery, contributing to improved efficiency and responsiveness.

5. Emissions

  • Euro 4 and Euro 5 Compliant: Many newer superbikes meet strict emissions standards, leading to cleaner engine operation and better fuel efficiency.
  • Fueling Technology: Technologies like variable valve timing (VVT) help reduce emissions and improve fuel economy.

4. Frame and Chassis

Lightweight aluminum or carbon fiber frames ensure agility and precise handling at high speeds.


1. Frame Material

  • Aluminum: Lightweight and strong, offering a good balance between weight and rigidity (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Honda CBR1000RR).
  • Carbon Fiber: Ultra-lightweight and extremely rigid, used in high-performance and premium models (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Steel Trellis: Offers high strength and rigidity, commonly used in Ducati and Aprilia superbikes for improved handling (e.g., Ducati Monster).
  • Chromoly Steel: Offers a balance between strength and weight, used in some superbikes for enhanced durability and performance.

2. Weight

  • Lightweight Designs: Superbikes typically weigh between 150 to 220 kg (curb weight), with some lightweight models tipping the scales at around 170 kg.
  • High-Performance Bikes: High-performance bikes like the Kawasaki Ninja H2R and Ducati Panigale V4 focus on reducing weight for better agility and handling.
  • Race-Ready Models: Carbon fiber and aluminum frame constructions help reduce weight, offering better track performance.

3. Wheelbase

  • Shorter Wheelbase (~1400mm-1450mm): Offers sharper handling, allowing for better cornering and quick changes in direction (common in sportier models like Yamaha YZF-R1).
  • Longer Wheelbase (~1450mm-1500mm): Improves stability at high speeds and is often found on touring or more stable handling bikes like the BMW S1000RR.

4. Chassis Design

  • Single-Sided Swingarm: Often used for aesthetic purposes and improved accessibility for wheel changes (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Double-Sided Swingarm: Standard design for greater strength and rigidity, providing more stability at high speeds (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Kawasaki ZX-10R).

5. Adjustable Geometry

  • Adjustable Steering Head Angle: Allows for better handling by modifying the rake and trail, often seen in race-oriented models.
  • Adjustable Ride Height: Provides the ability to modify the bike's center of gravity for different riding styles and track conditions.

6. Rigid vs. Flexible Frame

  • Rigid Frames: Provide maximum performance and responsiveness, commonly found in racing motorcycles where precision handling is crucial.
  • Flexible Frames: Offer improved comfort and stability, reducing rider fatigue on long rides and better performance in real-world conditions.

5. Suspension

Inverted telescopic forks and adjustable monoshock for optimal ride quality on different terrains.


1. Front Suspension

  • Inverted Telescopic Forks (USD Forks): Often used in high-performance superbikes, providing better rigidity and improved handling (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Honda CBR1000RR).
  • Conventional Telescopic Forks: Standard suspension used in entry-level and mid-range bikes, providing a balance between comfort and handling (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-6R).
  • Fully Adjustable Forks: Premium models come with fully adjustable forks (compression, rebound, and preload) to fine-tune the suspension based on rider preferences and track conditions (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4).

2. Rear Suspension

  • Monoshock: A single rear shock absorber that connects to the frame and rear swingarm, commonly used in superbikes for superior handling and weight reduction (e.g., Kawasaki ZX-10R, BMW S1000RR).
  • Dual Shock Absorbers: Found in some older or budget bikes, providing good comfort and stability, but not as precise as a monoshock setup.
  • Fully Adjustable Rear Shock: High-end bikes feature rear suspension that can be adjusted for preload, compression, and rebound damping to allow personalized tuning for various riding conditions.

3. Suspension Travel

  • Front Suspension Travel: Typically ranges from 100mm to 120mm in high-performance superbikes for better control during aggressive riding and cornering.
  • Rear Suspension Travel: Typically between 120mm to 135mm, offering a balance between comfort and stability at higher speeds.

4. Adjustability

  • Preload Adjustment: Allows the rider to adjust the amount of sag in the suspension, crucial for tuning based on rider weight and riding conditions.
  • Compression Damping: Controls how quickly the suspension compresses when hitting bumps, offering a smoother or firmer ride based on settings.
  • Rebound Damping: Controls how quickly the suspension returns to its normal position after compressing, preventing the bike from bouncing excessively after a bump.

5. Suspension Materials

  • Aluminum Forks: Lightweight material used in high-end superbike suspension forks for improved handling and rigidity (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja H2).
  • Carbon Fiber: Lightweight and highly rigid, carbon fiber is used in premium bikes for suspension components to reduce weight without compromising performance.

6. Suspension Technology

  • Electronic Suspension Adjustment (ESA): Advanced systems that automatically adjust the suspension settings based on road conditions or riding modes (e.g., BMW S1000RR with Dynamic ESA).
  • Ohlins Suspension: A renowned suspension brand known for premium, race-focused components that offer exceptional adjustability and performance.
  • Showa and KYB: Other leading brands that provide high-quality suspension components used in many superbikes for precision handling and comfort.

6. Brakes

Dual-disc front brakes with ABS and single-disc rear brakes for superior stopping power.


1. Front Brakes

  • Dual Disc Setup: Most superbikes use dual front discs (e.g., 320mm or 330mm) for superior braking power and heat dissipation (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Radial-Mounted Calipers: These calipers are mounted directly to the fork to improve braking performance and rigidity, providing more precise braking feel (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-10R).
  • Disc Material: High-performance discs often use materials like stainless steel or carbon composite for better heat resistance and reduced fade (e.g., Brembo discs).

2. Rear Brakes

  • Single Disc Setup: The rear brake typically uses a single 220mm to 250mm disc to provide stability and assist with braking balance (e.g., BMW S1000RR, Suzuki GSX-R1000).
  • Rear Calipers: High-performance rear calipers often feature a monoblock design, which helps to reduce flex and improve braking power.

3. Brake Technology

  • Anti-lock Braking System (ABS): Most modern superbikes are equipped with ABS to prevent wheel lockup during hard braking, enhancing rider safety (e.g., Honda CBR1000RR, Kawasaki Ninja H2).
  • Cornering ABS: An advanced version of ABS that takes lean angle into account, adjusting braking force to maintain stability during cornering (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4, BMW S1000RR).
  • Combined Braking System (CBS): This system applies a portion of the rear brake force when the front brake is used, improving stability and control (e.g., Honda CBR650R).

4. Brake Pads and Materials

  • Carbon-Carbon Pads: High-performance brake pads made from carbon composite materials that provide excellent heat resistance and stopping power in high-speed conditions.
  • Organic Pads: These are made from natural materials and offer quieter performance with good initial bite, suitable for street-oriented bikes.
  • Sintered Pads: Made from metal particles fused together, sintered pads provide excellent durability and stopping power, commonly found in superbikes.

5. Brake Fluid

  • Mineral Oil: Used in some motorcycles, providing smooth performance, but not as heat resistant as synthetic fluids.
  • DOT Brake Fluid: A common fluid used in superbikes, offering better high-temperature performance and superior braking under extreme conditions (e.g., DOT 4, DOT 5.1).

6. Brake Levers and Adjustability

  • Adjustable Brake Levers: Many premium superbikes feature adjustable levers for better ergonomics, allowing riders to fine-tune the lever position for comfort (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-10R).
  • Race-Style Levers: Some high-performance models come with race-style levers that are more responsive and easier to modulate during aggressive riding (e.g., Yamaha R1).

7. Tires

Wider rear radial tires for grip and narrower front tires for agility, ideal for high-performance riding.


1. Tire Types

  • Radial Tires: Most superbikes use radial tires for better performance and handling. These tires have a casing where the cord layers are positioned radially from the center of the tire (e.g., Michelin Pilot Power 2CT, Pirelli Diablo Supercorsa).
  • Bias Ply Tires: These are typically found on older bikes or entry-level models, offering durability but not the same level of performance as radial tires.

2. Tire Sizes

  • Front Tires: Typically range from 120mm to 190mm in width, with common sizes being 120/70 ZR17 (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Rear Tires: Larger rear tires, often ranging from 180mm to 200mm in width, with common sizes being 190/55 ZR17 or 200/55 ZR17 (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-10R, BMW S1000RR).
  • Superbike Tires: High-performance tires used in superbikes often have special compounds for enhanced grip, like the Pirelli Diablo Superbike, Bridgestone Battlax Racing, or Michelin Power Cup.

3. Tire Compound

  • Soft Compound: Offers maximum grip, ideal for track use or aggressive street riding. These tires wear out quickly but provide superior cornering and braking performance (e.g., Pirelli Diablo Supercorsa SP).
  • Medium Compound: A compromise between grip and longevity, providing a balance of performance for both street and track use (e.g., Bridgestone Battlax Hypersport S22).
  • Hard Compound: Prioritized for durability and long-distance riding. Not as grippy as softer compounds but offers better longevity for street-oriented superbikes (e.g., Michelin Pilot Road 5).

4. Tire Pressure

  • Front Tire Pressure: Typically between 32 to 36 PSI (depending on the bike and tire brand).
  • Rear Tire Pressure: Typically between 36 to 42 PSI for superbikes, with adjustments made depending on load and track conditions.
  • Importance: Proper tire pressure is essential for optimal grip, handling, and tire longevity. Incorrect tire pressure can affect the bike’s performance and safety.

5. Tire Tread

  • Track Tires: Often have a slick, smooth surface with minimal tread to maximize grip on dry track surfaces (e.g., Dunlop D212 GP Pro).
  • Street Tires: Designed with more tread grooves to provide better water dispersal and grip in wet conditions (e.g., Michelin Power RS, Pirelli Diablo Rosso III).
  • Mixed Use Tires: Offer a combination of road and track capabilities, with a balanced tread design for various riding conditions (e.g., Bridgestone Battlax S22, Pirelli Diablo Rosso Corsa).

6. Tire Brands

  • Pirelli: Known for its high-performance tires, especially the Pirelli Diablo series, designed for superbike-level grip and performance.
  • Michelin: Offers a wide range of tires, including the Michelin Power series, which is known for superb handling and grip on the road and track.
  • Bridgestone: Famous for their Battlax series, providing durable and high-performance tires for superbikes, including both road and track-focused options.
  • Dunlop: Known for its GP Pro series, Dunlop offers slick tires and high-performance options for racing and street use.

7. Tire Maintenance

  • Regular Inspections: It’s important to regularly inspect tires for signs of wear, damage, or uneven tread wear, as these can affect performance and safety.
  • Rotation: Rotating tires (if applicable) to ensure even wear can help extend tire life and improve performance.
  • Cleaning: Keeping tires clean from dirt and debris, especially after track days, helps maintain the tire surface and ensures optimal performance.

8. Performance

Capable of reaching 0-100 km/h in under 3 seconds, with top speeds exceeding 300 km/h.


1. Top Speed

  • Entry-Level Sports Bikes: Typically reach top speeds of around 160-200 km/h (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R3, Kawasaki Ninja 400).
  • Mid-Range Sports Bikes: Top speeds range from 200-250 km/h (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja ZX-6R, Suzuki GSX-R750).
  • Superbikes: Often exceed 300 km/h (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4, Yamaha YZF-R1, Kawasaki Ninja H2R).
  • Hyperbikes: The highest-performance motorcycles can exceed 350 km/h (e.g., Bugatti Veyron-inspired motorcycles, rare custom superbikes).

2. Acceleration

  • 0-100 km/h: Most superbikes can achieve this in under 3 seconds, with the fastest models like the Kawasaki Ninja H2R hitting 2.5 seconds.
  • Power-to-Weight Ratio: Superbikes have a very high power-to-weight ratio, meaning they can accelerate rapidly, especially in lower gears.
  • Launch Control: Many modern superbikes are equipped with launch control systems to optimize acceleration during racing or aggressive starts.

3. Torque

  • V-Twin Engines: Known for producing higher torque at lower RPMs, offering strong low-end power (e.g., Ducati Panigale V2, Aprilia RSV4).
  • Inline-Four Engines: Produce power more evenly across the RPM range but with slightly less low-end torque (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Suzuki GSX-R1000).
  • Supercharged Engines: Deliver massive torque and power, especially at higher RPMs, resulting in exhilarating acceleration (e.g., Kawasaki Ninja H2, H2R).

4. Handling

  • Lightweight Design: The lighter the bike, the quicker it responds to rider inputs, which is essential for tight cornering and quick maneuvering.
  • Suspension Setup: Superbikes typically come with adjustable suspension components to fine-tune handling for different tracks or roads (e.g., fully adjustable forks and shock absorbers).
  • Chassis Geometry: A shorter wheelbase and aggressive rake angles on superbikes help them handle sharp corners and tight turns efficiently.

5. Braking Performance

  • High-Performance Brake Systems: Brembo or similar high-performance calipers are used in superbikes, offering exceptional stopping power, especially at high speeds.
  • Carbon-Carbon Brakes: In hyperbikes, carbon-carbon brake systems are often employed for their lightweight properties and superior performance under high heat (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Anti-lock Braking Systems (ABS): Modern superbikes are equipped with cornering ABS, which helps prevent wheel lock-up and provides better control during aggressive braking.

6. Aerodynamics

  • Wind Tunnel Testing: Most modern superbikes undergo wind tunnel testing to ensure their aerodynamics are optimized for high-speed stability and reduced drag.
  • Bodywork: Lightweight fairings, integrated air ducts, and aggressive bodywork help superbikes cut through the air more efficiently.
  • Downforce: Advanced aerodynamics in hyperbikes or track-ready superbikes provide additional downforce, increasing stability at high speeds and through corners (e.g., Ducati Panigale V4).

7. Electronics and Performance Aids

  • Traction Control: Adjustable traction control settings allow riders to manage power delivery and prevent excessive wheel spin during acceleration (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1).
  • Power Modes: Riding modes such as Sport, Track, and Rain adjust throttle response, power delivery, and electronic settings for various riding conditions.
  • Quickshifter: Many superbikes are equipped with a quickshifter, which allows for clutchless gear shifts, providing faster acceleration and smoother gear transitions.
  • Launch Control: A system designed for optimal acceleration from a standstill, helping to prevent wheel spin and maximize speed during a launch.

9. Electronics and Features

Advanced electronics like traction control, quick shifters, and riding modes for enhanced usability.


1. Riding Modes

  • Sport Mode: Provides the most aggressive power delivery, optimized for track and high-performance riding.
  • Track Mode: Typically used for racing, offering sharp throttle response and minimal electronic intervention for maximum performance.
  • Rain Mode: Reduces power and provides more traction control to ensure safer riding in wet conditions.
  • Custom Mode: Allows the rider to personalize power delivery, traction control, and other settings for their preference or track conditions.

2. Traction Control

  • Adjustable Traction Control: Riders can adjust the level of traction control to suit different riding styles and conditions (e.g., Yamaha YZF-R1, Ducati Panigale V4).
  • Cornering ABS: Advanced traction control systems that help manage wheel slip and improve stability, especially during cornering and aggressive throttle application.
  • Wheelie Control: Some superbikes come with a wheelie control feature, which helps prevent the front wheel from lifting off the ground under hard acceleration.

3. Quickshifter

  • Seamless Gear Shifting: Allows for clutchless upshifts and downshifts, improving acceleration and making gear transitions smoother during aggressive riding.
  • Race-Inspired Technology: Often found in high-end superbikes, quickshifters allow for faster gear changes, especially on the track, reducing lap times and rider fatigue.

4. Launch Control

  • Optimized Launch: Helps the rider achieve the fastest start by controlling throttle and power delivery, especially in drag racing or track racing conditions.
  • Electronic Systems: Launch control is typically controlled by an onboard ECU, which adjusts the throttle input for maximum acceleration off the line.

5. Display and Connectivity

  • Full-Color TFT Displays: Most superbikes come with high-resolution TFT screens that show vital information such as speed, engine temperature, and RPM, often with customizable layouts.
  • Bluetooth and GPS: Some bikes come with Bluetooth connectivity for phone integration, navigation, and call control, as well as GPS features for route tracking and performance monitoring.
  • Data Logging: Advanced models allow data logging for performance metrics, track times, lap times, and other relevant racing data.

6. LED Lighting

  • LED Headlights: Provide brighter, more energy-efficient lighting compared to traditional halogen bulbs. Many superbikes have twin LED headlights for a sleek and aggressive look.
  • LED Taillights: More energy-efficient and offer better visibility, with some models incorporating dynamic lighting patterns for improved safety and aesthetics.
  • Daytime Running Lights (DRLs): Enhances visibility during the day, contributing to safer riding and giving the bike a modern appearance.

7. Anti-Lock Braking System (ABS)

  • Cornering ABS: A more advanced version of traditional ABS, this system adjusts braking force during cornering to prevent the wheels from locking up, ensuring better control under hard braking.
  • Electronic Braking Aids: Some superbikes feature electronic aids that help adjust brake pressure and prevent lock-ups even when aggressive braking forces are applied.

8. Hill Start Control

  • Prevents Roll-Back: This system holds the bike in place momentarily when starting on an incline, making it easier for the rider to manage throttle input and clutch release without rolling backward.
  • Electronic Assistance: Typically controlled via the bike's onboard electronics, this feature offers an additional layer of convenience for riders in hilly terrain.

10. Price Range

From $5,000 for entry-level sports bikes to over $100,000 for high-end superbikes.


1. Entry-Level Sports Bikes

  • Price Range: $5,000 - $10,000
  • Examples: Yamaha R3, KTM RC 390, Honda CBR500R, Kawasaki Ninja 400.
  • Engine Displacement: 300cc - 500cc
  • Target Audience: Riders looking for an affordable, beginner-friendly motorcycle with solid performance.

2. Mid-Range Sports Bikes

  • Price Range: $10,000 - $20,000
  • Examples: Yamaha YZF-R6, Kawasaki Ninja ZX-6R, Suzuki GSX-R750, Honda CBR650R.
  • Engine Displacement: 600cc - 750cc
  • Target Audience: Riders who want a more powerful sports bike with enhanced features, suitable for track days and spirited riding.

3. High-Performance Superbikes

  • Price Range: $20,000 - $50,000+
  • Examples: Yamaha YZF-R1, Ducati Panigale V4, Kawasaki Ninja ZX-10R, BMW S1000RR.
  • Engine Displacement: 1000cc+
  • Target Audience: Experienced riders seeking top-tier performance, cutting-edge technology, and track-ready bikes.

4. Hyperbikes (Top-Tier Models)

  • Price Range: $50,000 - $100,000+
  • Examples: Ducati Superleggera V4, Kawasaki Ninja H2R, BMW HP4 Race, MV Agusta F4 RC.
  • Engine Displacement: 1000cc - 1400cc+
  • Target Audience: Elite riders and collectors who demand the highest levels of performance, exclusive designs, and advanced engineering.
